Laplace (1788) showed that n_Io − 2n_Europa and n_Europa − 2n_Ganymede are commensurable to one part in 10⁻⁹, locking the three innermost Galilean moons into a stable orbital resonance. The angle φ_L librates tightly around 180° — a true triple conjunction (φ_L = 0) never occurs. Io's forced eccentricity (~0.0041) drives the tidal flexing responsible for its sulfur-fed volcanism. Callisto sits outside the resonance.

Why this matters

Tidal heating. If Io's orbit were a perfect circle, tidal stretching from Jupiter would be static — no heating. The Laplace resonance with Europa pumps Io's eccentricity up to ~0.0041, which causes the tidal bulge to flex by ~100 m every orbit. Dissipation of that flexing pours ~10¹⁴ W into Io's interior, more than a thousand times Earth's geothermal flux per unit area, powering the most volcanically active body in the solar system.

Stable for billions of years. Pierre-Simon Laplace proved the resonance dynamically stable in 1788. Numerical experiments confirm it has held since shortly after the moons formed; tidal evolution drove the system into the resonance, not out of it. The same physics binds Pluto–Charon, Saturn's Mimas–Tethys, and exoplanet systems like TRAPPIST-1.

What you're seeing. A pure Newtonian Yoshida-4 integration in barycentric SI units. No fudge, no special-case forcing — the resonance falls out of pairwise gravity alone. Energy and angular momentum are bounded (symplectic) so the integration stays physical for arbitrarily long runs.
